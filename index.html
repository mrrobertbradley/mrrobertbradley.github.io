About Me

I've spent twenty-two years teaching Computer Science in almost every setting the sector offers — further and higher education, traditional secondary schools, UTCs, adult and community education, and alternative provision. That breadth has shaped a simple conviction: computing is best taught as a way of thinking, with code as the medium rather than the subject.

I'm currently Head of Computer Science at The Abbey School in Reading, where I also serve as Paget Housemaster. Since joining, departmental outcomes have been transformed: GCSE grade 9 results have risen from 15.6% to 48%, with 100% A*–B grades maintained at A-Level. As a department in a girls' school, I'm particularly proud of our record in getting more girls into Computer Science and keeping them there — work recognised with the SheCanCode STEM Educator Award, and which I regularly share at GSA and Computing at School events.

My curriculum design leans on what works in front of real students: Minecraft Education-based Python programming, physical computing with micro:bits, algorithms-first sequencing, and a spiral approach that revisits ideas with growing depth. I'm a Founding Fellow of the Chartered College of Teaching, a Member of the Society for Education and Training, and an ex-Microsoft Innovative Educator Expert; and I previously led UTC Reading to Microsoft Showcase School status four years running, alongside whole-school digital strategies covering AI policy, one-to-one devices, and digital examination pilots.

Teacher Development

Developing other teachers matters as much to me as teaching students. I've served as ITT Coordinator, PGCE and NQT mentor, and STEM Facilitator on the NCCE's CS Accelerator programme, and I take genuine satisfaction in seeing colleagues go on to leadership roles, international speaking opportunities, and recognition as subject experts in their own right.

Beyond the Classroom

My interests run to cybersecurity and open-source intelligence — fields I've followed since long before they had qualifications attached, and which keep my subject knowledge sharper than any textbook could. I put that interest to practical use as a volunteer, applying open-source intelligence techniques to cold missing persons cases.
